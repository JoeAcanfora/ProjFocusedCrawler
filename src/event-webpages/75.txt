Typhoon Haiyan survivors: what they saved in the storm – in pictures









To mark the first anniversary of typhoon Haiyan, the Catholic aid agency Cafod visited survivors in the Philippines. These images, taken by Lukasz Cholewiak, show the few precious things they were able to save












Guardian Global development is supported by:

Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation

Flora, 39 – my children’s birth certificatesFlora lives by the coast in Guiuan, the first place hit by the typhoon. As waves flooded the village, her home was washed away. Flora managed to save her children’s birth certificates. ‘They’re important because my children can’t register for school without them,’ she explains

John Manuel, 15 – my dog, BrunoAs John’s siblings fled their home on the island of Panay, he stayed to save Bruno. It was lucky for his neighbour, who is partly paralysed, that he did. John saved him by keeping his head above water. He says: ‘I just held him, even when he passed out. I was crying and praying. Then my dad came back. He saved us’

Romila, 37 – a towelWhen the typhoon hit, Romila, who was eight months pregnant, raced to the local church with her husband. As they took shelter, their home began to collapse. ‘It was very difficult to run. My stomach was bulging,’ she says. ‘We had to leave everything behind. The only thing I could take was a towel, which I used to cover my head’

Leejohn, five – my toysThanks to his grandmother, Leejohn still had toys to play with after the storm. She had wrapped them in a plastic bag. After the roof of the house fell in, rain poured in and floodwater flowed under the door. ‘Our hearts were racing,’ says Leejohn’s grandfather. ‘If the flood had come much higher we would have died’

Leopoldo, 66 – a tin of riceAfter taking his family over a river to higher ground, Leopoldo returned home for a tin of rice. But as he recrossed the bridge, he was knocked over by a wave. ‘I nearly drowned,’ he says. ‘But I was able to grab a branch. I managed to pull myself up to the road.’ He held on to the tin, but the soaking rice was unusable

Amalia, 83 – a Minnie Mouse bag  Amalia and her son sheltered at home before having to flee. ‘I thought typhoon Yolanda [as it was called locally] would be like other typhoons, but it was like four in one,’ Amalia says. ‘When I fled our home, I took a small bag, with Minnie Mouse on the front. I grabbed it in a panic and stuffed it with clothes’

Corazon, 71 – my rosary As her home on Leyte island fell down, Corazon clung to a rosary. ‘I thought it was the end. All I did was pray with my rosary,’ she says. ‘I was injured and I felt numb. I was trapped underneath the debris. My nephews pulled me out and carried me away. I wasn’t able to walk because I was shaking and trembling’

Ronald, 34 – my wife, Maria Gracia The night before the storm stuck, Ronald had taken his wife, Maria Gracia, to hospital on Leyte island. Early in the morning, the hospital roof was blown off. As he carried her away, her feet became caught in a mosquito net. He dived under the water to free her and then took her safely to the next building
